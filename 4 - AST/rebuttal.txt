We would like to thank all reviewers for their careful reading and helpful comments. Below, we give answers to the main questions raised.

Review #99A.
We thank the reviewer for the helpful suggestions. Concerning the request for an example illustrating the limitations of the calculus, a natural candidate is the well-known coupon collector process, a classical example in probabilistic termination. This process can be encoded in our calculus as a sequential composition of $n$ loops (where $n$ is the number of coupons$, each loops having a decreasing exit probability. However, it cannot currently be expressed using two nested loop within our system, due to the restriction of a fixed exit probability. We agree that including such an example would improve the discussion, and we will incorporate it in a revised version. We also appreciate the remark regarding the order of the antecedent vector in the typing rules. We acknowledge that this point may be confusing, and we will revise the presentation 

Review #99B.
We sincerely thank the reviewer for the detailed feedback and for acknowledging the relevance of the topic. We fully agree that the paper would benefit from a broader and more balanced discussion of related work. In particular, we acknowledge that references to the works of Bournez and collaborators, as well as Saheb-Djahromi, should be included in relation to AST and PAST, and we will add them in a revised version. We also agree that Ehrhard’s work is closely related in spirit, especially regarding the use of an external source of randomness in the underlying operational model. While there are clear structural similarities between the underlying machines, the perspectives differ: our work is primarily focused on a type-theoretic approach to termination, whereas Ehrhard’s work is fundamentally rooted in denotational semantics. We will expand the discussion to better clarify these connections. Finally, we appreciate the suggestion to better motivate the use of the FMC over other abstract machines, and we will include additional discussion to make this design choice clearer. (Maybe we can say that we can capture all reduction strategies?)

Review #99C.
We thank the reviewer for the positive evaluation and insightful comments. We agree that establishing decidability results for type checking (or type inference) in the full calculus would be highly desirable. At present we are actively investigating it, and we hope to report progress on this problem in future work.
